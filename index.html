Confidence Intervals A confidence interval is two points (the lower and upper limits) between which the unknown population quantity being estimated lies with a certain probability.

Heuristic Formula 𝑷𝒐𝒊𝒏𝒕 𝑬𝒔𝒕𝒊𝒎𝒂𝒕𝒆 ± (𝑫𝒊𝒔𝒕𝒓𝒊𝒃𝒖𝒕𝒊𝒐𝒏 𝑷𝒆𝒓𝒄𝒆𝒏𝒕𝒊𝒍𝒆) × (𝑺𝒕𝒂𝒏𝒅𝒂𝒓𝒅 𝑬𝒓𝒓𝒐𝒓)

  • Each of the quantities – the point estimate, the percentile, and the standard error – depends on what is being estimated. Consult the formulas on the following pages to see how.
  • The percentile additionally depends on the level of confidence, (e.g.) 90%, 95%, 99%, etc.
  • Remember that this heuristic formula does not apply for confidence intervals for variances and ratios of variances.

Interpretation

The confidence interval contains the unknown population quantity (mean, difference in means, proportion, difference in proportions, etc.) being estimated with a certain probability. More practically speaking, points inside the confidence interval represent “plausible” values for the unknown population quantity. Points outside the confidence interval are not plausible. Confidence intervals are often used with “benchmark” values for the quantity being estimated. If the benchmark lies in the confidence interval, then the benchmark is a plausible value. If the benchmark lies outside the confidence interval, then the benchmark is not a plausible value.

Hypothesis Tests

The Hypothesis Testing Process

Know and be able to execute (most) the steps of the hypothesis testing process:

  1. State a research hypothesis
  2. Define and evaluate the data
  3. Refine a research hypothesis to a statistical hypothesis Identify a suitable test statistic
  4. Determine reference distribution for the test statistic State the statistical decision rule
  5. Evaluate assumptions about the data
  6. Calculate the test statistic
  7. Make the statistical decision
  8. Calculate p-value and confidence interval

Heuristic Formula

$$\text{Test Statistic}=\frac{\text {Point Estimate-Null Value}}{Standard Error}$$
